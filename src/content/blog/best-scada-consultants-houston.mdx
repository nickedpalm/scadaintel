## What to Actually Ask Before Hiring

Most RFPs for SCADA work ask the wrong questions. "How many years have you been in business?" is far less useful than "Can you name the last three systems you migrated and what the primary integration challenges were?"

Here's a short list of questions that separate real OT consultants from IT generalists:

**On credentials:**
- Do your engineers hold GICSP, CAP, or ISA/IEC 62443 certifications?
- What OEM platform certifications do your PLC/HMI programmers carry?

**On experience:**
- What's your experience with NERC CIP compliance reviews in Texas?
- Have you worked on SCADA systems under API 1164 (pipeline security) or NIST SP 800-82 guidelines?

**On scope:**
- What does your standard deliverable package include — just code, or architecture diagrams, network segmentation docs, and FAT/SAT test plans?
- How do you handle OEM warranty implications when you modify a legacy system?

> **Pro Tip:** Ask for a redacted copy of a previous architecture diagram or vulnerability assessment. Firms that have done real work will have these on file. Firms that haven't will give you a brochure instead.

## The Cybersecurity Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About

Houston's industrial facilities are high-value targets. In 2021, a threat actor accessed the SCADA system of a water treatment plant in Florida by exploiting remote access software — and that attack was on a facility an order of magnitude less critical than most Houston-area plants.

Any SCADA engagement today that doesn't include a cybersecurity component is incomplete. Full stop.

What that looks like in practice:
- **OT network segmentation** — separating control system networks from corporate IT and the internet
- **Asset inventory** — you can't secure what you don't know you have
- **Vulnerability assessment** — identifying unpatched firmware, default credentials, and exposed interfaces
- **NERC CIP compliance** (for utilities) or **ISA/IEC 62443** framework alignment (for everyone else)

Firms like Braintek, which focuses specifically on oil-and-gas SCADA security and field connectivity, understand this environment. A generic managed security provider does not.

## How Houston Differs From Other Markets

If you've worked with SCADA consultants in other regions, a few things will stand out here:

**The oil & gas bias is real.** Many Houston SCADA firms are deeply optimized for upstream and midstream energy — wellhead automation, custody transfer metering, pipeline SCADA. If your facility is a water treatment plant or a food processing operation, explicitly confirm that the firm has non-energy sector experience before engaging.

**Remote operations are the norm.** Houston-area SCADA systems frequently need to connect to field sites across Texas and into the Gulf. Firms operating here should be fluent in VSAT, cellular failover, and secure remote access protocols — not just local LAN/WAN design.

**Turnaround windows are real constraints.** Refineries operate on scheduled turnarounds, and control system work often has to slot into narrow maintenance windows. Firms that can't staff 24/7 support during a two-week turnaround aren't the right fit for that kind of project.
